## 1. Problem Overview & Domain Context

Pharmacy intervention (PI) documents are free-text clinical records generated by clinical pharmacists during medication order review. They document drug-related problems (DRPs) — prescription errors, dosing issues, therapeutic duplications, contraindications, inadequate monitoring — and the resulting pharmacist recommendations. Automatically classifying these documents into one of **five mutually exclusive intervention categories** at point-of-submission enables:

- Real-time decision support and routing
- Retrospective quality audits and safety analytics
- Surveillance of drug-related problem trends
- Resource allocation and pharmacovigilance

**Key domain characteristics driving model selection:**

| Characteristic | Implication |
|---|---|
| Short-to-medium free-text documents | Standard BERT-class context window (512 tokens) usually sufficient |
| Dense clinical/drug terminology | Requires domain-adapted pre-training |
| 5-class mutually exclusive setup | Softmax classification head; macro-F1 preferred |
| Likely class imbalance (rare intervention types) | Requires imbalance-aware training |
| High-stakes clinical deployment | Interpretability, calibration, and confidence thresholds critical |
| Institutional/proprietary data | Privacy constraints; on-premise or HIPAA-compliant infrastructure |

**Prior Art in This Exact Space:** Alkanj et al. (2025, *Eur J Hosp Pharm*) demonstrated that deep neural network classifiers can automatically classify DRPs from PI free text in a large French university hospital database, achieving competitive performance across an 11-category SFPC classification scheme without requiring substantial human labelling overhead. This validates the technical feasibility of the present approach.

## 2. Recommended Model Families with Rationale

### 2.1 Domain-Adapted BERT Variants (Primary Recommendation)

**Models:** BioBERT, PubMedBERT, BioClinicalBERT, BioClinical ModernBERT

**Why suitable:** BERT-family encoder models have become the de-facto standard for clinical text classification. Pre-training on biomedical literature (PubMed abstracts, PMC full-text) and/or clinical EHR notes (MIMIC-III) gives these models a substantially richer vocabulary and contextual representations for drug names, clinical concepts, dosing language, and pharmacological terminology compared to general-domain BERT.

- **PubMedBERT** (Gu et al., 2021) is trained from scratch on PubMed text rather than fine-tuned from general BERT, making it particularly well-suited for biomedical documents that skew toward journal-style or structured clinical language.
- **BioClinicalBERT** (Alsentzer et al., 2019) uniquely bridges biomedical literature and clinical notes (MIMIC-III), making it highly appropriate when PI documents contain EHR-style free text alongside pharmacological concepts.
- **BioClinical ModernBERT** (2025) extends the architecture with a context window up to 8,192 tokens (via RoPE), flash attention, and training on two trillion tokens — the current state-of-the-art encoder for clinical NLP tasks requiring long-context or high efficiency.

**Classification mechanism:** Add a `[CLS]`-token classification head (dense + softmax over 5 classes); fine-tune end-to-end on labelled PI documents with cross-entropy (or focal) loss.

---

### 2.2 RoBERTa and Its Biomedical Variants

**Models:** RoBERTa-base/large, BioMed-RoBERTa, RoBERTa-large-PM-M3-Voken

**Why suitable:** RoBERTa improves upon BERT by optimising pre-training with dynamic masking, larger batch sizes, longer training durations, and removal of the Next Sentence Prediction objective. Studies in clinical NLP consistently show RoBERTa achieving higher F1 scores on classification tasks compared to base BERT, especially on short-to-medium documents. Its biomedical variants are trained on PubMed and PubMed Central, making vocabulary coverage for drug/intervention text superior.

---

### 2.3 Large Language Models — Fine-Tuned (Instruction-Tuned)

**Models:** Llama 3.1/3.2 (8B, fine-tuned), Mistral-7B, BioMedLM, Med42

**Why suitable:** Recent systematic reviews in *JMIR Medical Informatics* and *JAMIA* (2024–2025) confirm that while LLMs under zero-shot/few-shot prompting underperform compared to supervised fine-tuned BERT-class models on classification tasks, fine-tuned smaller LLMs (7–13B parameters) with supervised fine-tuning (SFT) can match or exceed encoder-only performance on clinical text classification. They are particularly valuable when label data is limited or when the task requires capturing longer contextual dependencies.

---

### 2.4 Large Language Models — Zero/Few-Shot (GPT-4, Claude)

**Models:** GPT-4o, Claude 3.5/3.7, Gemini 1.5 Pro

**Why suitable (specific use cases):** The *JAMIA* (2024) comparative evaluation showed GPT-4 zero-shot classifiers achieving higher recall than supervised NLP models on several health classification tasks, while supervised PLMs achieved higher F1. Practically, these are useful for:
- Generating synthetic training data for underrepresented classes
- Bootstrapping weak labels for pre-annotation
- Ensemble soft-labelling pipeline

**Important caveat:** For production deployment, PHI/PII considerations and HIPAA compliance must be carefully assessed before routing pharmacy documents to commercial API endpoints.

---

### 2.5 Traditional ML with TF-IDF / Clinical Word Embeddings

**Models:** Logistic Regression, SVM (linear kernel), XGBoost with TF-IDF or Word2Vec/FastText features

**Why suitable:** In data-scarce settings (< 500 labelled examples per class), classical ML often outperforms neural models due to lower overfitting risk. They also provide strong, interpretable baselines and clinical feature importance via model weights. Studies in clinical NLP (e.g., Ling et al., 2023; JMIR 2025 NLP pharmaceutical records study) confirm these remain competitive in low-resource scenarios.

---

### 2.6 Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) over Word Embeddings

**Models:** TextCNN (Kim 2014), DPCNN, CharCNN

**Why suitable:** TextCNN models are efficient, fast-training classifiers that capture local n-gram patterns highly relevant to pharmacy text (e.g., "dose adjustment recommended", "contraindicated in renal failure"). They serve as strong intermediate baselines between traditional ML and Transformer models and have been successfully applied in adverse drug reaction NLP tasks.

## 3. Pros & Cons Comparison

| Model | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| **PubMedBERT / BioClinicalBERT** | Best domain alignment; proven SOTA on clinical classification; moderate compute | Needs ~200–500 labelled examples per class to fine-tune reliably; 512-token limit |
| **BioClinical ModernBERT** | Long context (8K tokens); modern architecture; most efficient BERT-class model for clinical NLP (2025 SOTA) | Less mature; fewer published benchmarks; higher compute than base BERT |
| **RoBERTa / BioMed-RoBERTa** | Robust pre-training; consistently strong F1; less sensitive to hyperparameters than BERT | Biomedical variants less fine-tuned on clinical notes specifically |
| **Fine-tuned LLM (Llama/Mistral)** | Handles complex reasoning; can work with limited labels via instruction tuning; good generalisation | High GPU memory requirements; slow inference; risk of hallucinated confidence |
| **GPT-4 / Claude (zero-shot)** | No fine-tuning required; useful for label generation; high recall | Lower F1 than fine-tuned models; API cost; PHI/HIPAA risk; non-deterministic |
| **Logistic Regression + TF-IDF** | Interpretable; fast; effective with small data; easy to audit | No contextual understanding; misses synonyms and paraphrasing |
| **SVM (linear kernel)** | Strong generalisation; well-calibrated; low variance | Cannot model complex multi-word clinical patterns |
| **TextCNN** | Captures local n-grams; fast training; GPU efficient | No long-range dependency modelling; less interpretable than logistic regression |
| **XGBoost + embeddings** | Handles feature engineering well; robust to class imbalance via sample weights | Requires explicit feature engineering; not end-to-end |

## 4. SOTA Approaches (2023–2025)

### 4.1 Domain-Specific Encoder SOTA

**BioClinical ModernBERT (Warner et al. / adapted 2025):** Extends ModernBERT's 8,192-token context, flash attention, and two-trillion-token pre-training with sequential adaptation on PubMed and MIMIC clinical notes. It is currently the leading publicly available encoder model for biomedical and clinical NLP, achieving SOTA on multiple classification, NER, and similarity benchmarks. For PI documents that may include medication histories or patient contexts, this model eliminates chunking artefacts from 512-token limits.

### 4.2 Weak Supervision + Domain-Adapted PLM

Combining rule-based NLP labellers (e.g., regex over PCNE/SFPC drug-problem ontologies) with BERT-class models under weak supervision reduces annotation costs dramatically. Studies such as the clinical phenotype classification paradigm (Lependu et al., NCBI/PMC) demonstrate that weak labels + BERT representation learning can achieve F1 scores within 5–8 points of fully supervised models.

### 4.3 LLM-Assisted Annotation + Encoder Fine-Tuning

The *JAMIA* 2024 evaluation (Zong et al.) showed that supervised NLP models trained on **GPT-4-annotated data** outperformed models trained on GPT-3.5-annotated data across most classification tasks. This pipeline — GPT-4 generates soft labels → BioClinicalBERT fine-tuned on these labels → deployed encoder for inference — is increasingly adopted as a scalable low-human-effort SOTA approach.

### 4.4 Fine-Tuned Small LLMs

Fine-tuned Llama 3.1 (8B) with SFT on clinical classification datasets has been shown (MedSlice, 2025; Med42, 2024) to be competitive with encoder-only BERT models while offering generative explanations alongside classifications — a key advantage for clinical adoption and interpretability.

### 4.5 Hybrid Frameworks

Edinburgh Clinical NLP (MEDIQA-CORR 2024) demonstrated that combining LLM-generated hints/reasoning chains with discriminative encoder classifiers (BioLinkBERT) yields better performance than either approach in isolation. For PI classification, this could manifest as: GPT-4 generates a chain-of-thought rationale → rationale + original document fed to BioClinicalBERT for final class prediction.

## 6. Interpretable Models for Clinical Settings

Clinical pharmacists and hospital administrators require model transparency for regulatory compliance, clinical governance, and trust. Recommended strategies:

### 6.1 Inherently Interpretable Models

- **Logistic Regression with TF-IDF:** Coefficient weights directly reveal which drug names, problem descriptors, or action phrases drive each category prediction. Highly auditable.
- **Regularised linear SVM:** Similar advantage; sparse weight vectors highlight key tokens.
- **Decision Tree / Rule Induction (RIPPER, PART):** Explicit if-then rule sets aligned with pharmacist mental models of DRP classification.

### 6.2 Post-Hoc Explanation for Deep Models

- **SHAP (SHapley Additive Explanations):** Provides both local (per-document) and global (corpus-level) feature attribution. Identifies which words/phrases drove the classification decision. Preferred over LIME for consistency and theoretical grounding.
- **LIME:** Local surrogate model for individual predictions; computationally lighter than SHAP; good for rapid auditing of individual pharmacy documents.
- **Integrated Gradients:** Gradient-based attribution native to Transformer models; highlights input tokens most responsible for the prediction.
- **Attention Weight Visualisation:** BertViz-style attention heatmaps enable pharmacists to see which drug names, phrases, and clinical concepts the model attends to — though attention weights should not be treated as ground-truth explanations.

### 6.3 Calibration for Clinical Trust

Model confidence scores must be **calibrated** (outputs reflect true probabilities) before deployment. Apply **temperature scaling** or **Platt scaling** post-training. A well-calibrated model allows pharmacists to set confidence thresholds — for example, routing predictions below 70% confidence to human review.

> **Recommendation for clinical deployment:** Lead with a fine-tuned BioClinicalBERT + SHAP explanations pipeline. Present class probabilities alongside top influential tokens per prediction. Flag low-confidence predictions for pharmacist review rather than auto-classifying.

## 7. Publication-Worthy Advanced Models

For researchers targeting journals such as *JAMIA*, *Journal of Biomedical Informatics*, *Eur J Hosp Pharm*, or ACL BioNLP Workshop:

### 7.1 Multi-Task Learning with Auxiliary Objectives

Train a single model to simultaneously predict (a) the 5-class intervention category and (b) auxiliary structured outputs such as drug name, problem type, severity, or accepted/rejected outcome. Multi-task learning regularises the encoder, often improving generalisation on smaller pharmacy datasets.

### 7.2 Contrastive Learning for Rare Classes

Apply supervised contrastive loss (SupCon) during fine-tuning to explicitly separate minority class embeddings from majority classes in the representation space. This has shown large gains on imbalanced medical classification tasks and is an underexplored angle in pharmacy NLP.

### 7.3 Retrieval-Augmented Classification (RAC)

Augment each PI document at inference time with semantically similar examples from a curated knowledge base (e.g., PCNE classification database, previous labelled PIs). The retrieved context + input document is passed to the classifier. This is particularly powerful for novel or rare intervention types and provides an interpretable evidence trail.

### 7.4 LLM Chain-of-Thought + Encoder Ensemble

Replicate the Edinburgh NLP MEDIQA approach in the PI domain: use GPT-4 or Claude to generate a step-by-step pharmacological reasoning trace, concatenate with the original PI text, and fine-tune a domain BERT encoder on the augmented inputs. The reasoning trace acts as a form of curriculum-guided data augmentation.

### 7.5 Self-Training with Pseudo-Labels

Bootstrap from a small seed labelled set → train initial BioClinicalBERT → generate pseudo-labels for unlabelled PI documents → retrain on expanded set with confidence thresholds. This semi-supervised approach is practically valuable given the cost of pharmacist annotation.

### 7.6 Adapter-Based Domain Continual Pre-Training

Rather than full fine-tuning of BioClinicalBERT on in-hospital PI documents, train lightweight adapter layers (LoRA or Houlsby adapters) on in-domain unlabelled PI text before the supervised classification phase. This is computationally efficient, preserves the original model's biomedical knowledge, and enables institution-specific adaptation without full re-training.

## 9. Key Risks

### 9.1 Overfitting

**Risk:** High, especially with small labelled datasets and large pre-trained models.

**Mitigations:**
- Freeze lower BERT layers during early fine-tuning epochs; apply gradual unfreezing
- Apply aggressive dropout (0.2–0.3) on the classification head
- Use weight decay (L2 regularisation) during AdamW optimisation
- Early stopping on validation macro-F1 (not loss)
- Cross-validate using stratified k-fold (k=5) to detect instability
- Use LoRA/adapter fine-tuning to reduce trainable parameter count

---

### 9.2 Data Leakage

**Risk:** Temporal or patient-level leakage can inflate performance metrics by 10–20+ points.

**Specific pharmacy risks:**
- Same patient appearing in train and test splits (patient-level leakage)
- Pharmacist writing style idiosyncrasies leaking label information (annotator bias)
- Temporal distribution shift: PI categories may shift over time as drug formularies and guidelines change — a temporally naïve random split overestimates deployment performance

**Mitigations:**
- Always perform **patient-level splits** (not document-level)
- Use **temporal holdout sets** (train on earlier dates, test on most recent quarter)
- Remove pharmacist identifiers before training to avoid annotator fingerprinting
- Implement **prospective evaluation** (simulated real-time classification)

---

### 9.3 Class Imbalance

**Risk:** In real pharmacy intervention corpora, common categories (e.g., "dose adjustment") vastly outnumber rare ones (e.g., "contraindication"). Models trained with standard cross-entropy tend to systematically misclassify minority classes.

**Mitigations (tiered):**

| Strategy | Mechanism | Recommended For |
|---|---|---|
| **Class-weighted cross-entropy** | Upweight minority classes in loss function | Always apply as default |
| **Focal Loss** (Lin et al., 2017) | Down-weight easy majority examples; focus on hard minority examples | Particularly effective when imbalance ratio > 5:1 |
| **Oversampling (EDA / back-translation)** | Augment minority class documents via synonym replacement, paraphrase, translation | Moderate imbalance |
| **LLM-based synthetic data generation** | GPT-4 generates realistic PI documents for underrepresented classes, reviewed by a pharmacist | Severe imbalance (< 50 examples) |
| **SMOTE on embeddings** | Interpolate in BioClinicalBERT embedding space (not raw text) | Experimental; benchmarked 2025 |
| **Threshold adjustment** | Move classification threshold per class based on cost matrix | Deployment calibration |

> **SMOTE caveat:** Standard SMOTE on TF-IDF vectors is a common practice but has theoretical limitations for text. A 2025 PMC benchmarking study (Karayiğit et al.) confirmed that SMOTE variants improve balanced accuracy but results are highly task- and representation-dependent. For Transformer-based models, operate in embedding space or use linguistic augmentation instead.

## 10. Evaluation Metrics

Given the 5-class imbalanced classification setting with clinical impact considerations:

| Metric | Rationale | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| **Macro-averaged F1** | Treats all 5 classes equally regardless of support; critical for minority class performance | **Primary** |
| **Weighted F1** | Accounts for class frequency; reflects overall system performance | Secondary |
| **Per-class Precision, Recall, F1** | Enables identification of which specific intervention categories are hardest to classify | **Always report** |
| **Cohen's Kappa (κ)** | Measures agreement between model and pharmacist gold labels beyond chance; required for clinical validation studies | Clinical validation |
| **AUROC (macro-averaged OvR)** | Threshold-free discrimination; useful for calibration analysis and confidence thresholds | Deployment |
| **Balanced Accuracy** | Mean recall across all classes; robust alternative to macro-F1 | Supplement |
| **Calibration (ECE / Reliability Diagram)** | Essential for clinical deployment — confidence scores must reflect true probabilities | Deployment |
| **Confusion Matrix** | Reveals systematic confusions between adjacent intervention categories | Always report |

> **What NOT to use as primary metric:** Accuracy (highly misleading under class imbalance). If the dominant class holds 40% of documents, a majority classifier achieves 40% accuracy — meaningless in this setting.

## 11. Novelty Contribution Ideas

The following research directions represent viable publication contributions not yet comprehensively addressed in the pharmacy NLP literature:

### 11.1 Cross-Institutional Transfer Learning
Most pharmacy NLP models are trained and tested at a single institution. Train on one hospital's PI documents, evaluate zero-shot and few-shot transfer to a different institution with different DRP classification schemes (e.g., PCNE vs. SFPC). Quantifying and reducing this distribution shift would be a significant practical contribution.

### 11.2 Hierarchical Classification of Intervention Documents
The SFPC classification has 11 top-level categories and 29 sub-categories. A hierarchical classification model (predict category first, then sub-type) versus flat multi-class model comparison has not been rigorously benchmarked on pharmacy intervention text.

### 11.3 Temporal Concept Drift Monitoring
Drug formularies, clinical guidelines, and prescribing patterns change over time, causing a PI classifier's real-world performance to degrade. Developing and evaluating **concept drift detection** frameworks for pharmacy NLP classifiers is an underexplored but clinically critical area.

### 11.4 Pharmacist Confidence Calibration Study
Empirically compare pharmacist inter-rater agreement (Cohen's κ on PI documents) with model confidence and accuracy — establishing a human performance ceiling and benchmarking model uncertainty estimation against it. Few clinical NLP papers rigorously establish the human performance baseline.

### 11.5 Privacy-Preserving Federated Learning
Train PI classifiers across multiple hospitals without sharing patient data using federated learning (e.g., Flower framework + BioClinicalBERT LoRA adapters). This is a novel, high-impact, publishable direction given GDPR and HIPAA constraints in healthcare NLP.

### 11.6 LLM-Generated Explanations as Clinical Decision Support
Rather than just classifying the PI document, generate a natural-language justification aligned with PCNE/SFPC terminology. Evaluate whether these justifications (a) improve pharmacist acceptance of model decisions and (b) agree with gold-standard clinical rationale — a novel human factors contribution.

### 11.7 Active Learning for Annotation Efficiency
Evaluate query strategies (uncertainty sampling, diversity sampling, BADGE) for efficiently selecting PI documents for pharmacist annotation. Demonstrate comparable classification performance to fully supervised models with 30–50% of the labelled data — directly addressing real-world annotation bottlenecks.

## 12. Common Evaluation Benchmarks

While no single public benchmark exists for pharmacy intervention classification specifically, the following adjacent benchmarks are used for pre-training and evaluation comparison in clinical NLP:

| Benchmark | Task Type | Domain Relevance |
|---|---|---|
| **BLURB** (Gu et al., 2021) | NER, RE, QA, document classification | Standard biomedical NLP leaderboard; used to validate PubMedBERT |
| **MIMIC-III / MIMIC-IV** (PhysioNet) | ICD code classification, readmission prediction, NER | Gold-standard clinical text; discharge summaries include medication information |
| **n2c2 NLP Challenges** (2006–2023) | Smoking status, medication extraction, ADE, relations | Closest to pharmacy notes; several challenges cover medication-level classification |
| **MedNLI** | Clinical natural language inference | Tests clinical language understanding |
| **BioASQ** | QA over biomedical literature | Pre-training evaluation for biomedical models |
| **CLEF eHealth** | ICD coding, patient note classification | Multi-lingual clinical document classification |
| **PCNE DRP Dataset** | Drug-related problem classification | Direct domain analog if available; used in European pharmacy informatics |
| **SFPC Pharmaceutical Intervention Database** | PI classification (French) | Closest existing benchmark to this task (see Alkanj et al., 2025) |

> **Key note:** For novel institutional PI datasets, always compare against **both** (a) a human inter-rater agreement baseline and (b) at least one public benchmark task to contextualise model capability.
